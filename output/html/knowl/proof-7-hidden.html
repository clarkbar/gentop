Assume (1); we aim to prove (2). Let \(T \subseteq Y\text{,}\) and assume that \(x\) is close to \(f^{-1}(T)\text{.}\) Then by assumption \(f(x)\) is close to \(f(f^{-1}(T))\text{.}\) Since \(f(f^{-1}(T)) \subseteq T\text{,}\) it follows that \(f(x)\) is close to \(T\text{,}\) so \(x \in f^{-1}(\tau_Y(T))\text{.}\)

Assume (2); we aim to prove (3). Let \(Z \subseteq Y\) be closed. Then

\begin{equation*} \tau_X(f^{-1}(Z)) \subseteq f^{-1}(\tau_Y(Z)) = f^{-1}(Z)\text{,} \end{equation*}

so \(f^{-1}(Z)\) is closed.

Assume (3); then (4) follows by taking complements.

Assume (4); we aim to prove (5). Let \(x \in X\text{,}\) and let \(\varepsilon > 0\text{.}\) The subset \(f^{-1}(B^n(f(x),\varepsilon)\cap Y) \subseteq X\) is open. Consequently, there exists \(\delta>0\) such that \(B^m(x,\delta)\cap X \subseteq f^{-1}(B^n(f(x),\varepsilon)\cap Y)\text{;}\) that is, for any \(x_0 \in X\) such that \(d(x_0,x)\lt \delta\text{,}\) one has \(d(f(x_0),f(x))\lt \varepsilon\text{,}\) as desired.

Assume (5); we aim to prove (1). Let \(S \subseteq X\text{,}\) and let \(x\in X\) be a point that is close to \(S\text{.}\) Let \(\varepsilon > 0\text{.}\) By assumption there is a \(\delta>0\) such that for any \(x_0 \in X\) with \(d(x_0,x)\lt \delta\text{,}\) we have \(d(f(x_0),f(x))\lt \varepsilon\text{.}\) Since \(x\) is close to \(S\text{,}\) we may select \(s \in S\) such that \(d(x,s) \lt \delta\text{.}\) Thus \(d(f(x),f(s))\lt \varepsilon\text{.}\) This proves that \(f(x)\) is close to \(f(S)\text{.}\)
