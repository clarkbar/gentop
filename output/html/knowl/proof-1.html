Proof

Let us prove these in order.

  1. If \(x \in S\text{,}\) then for every \(\varepsilon>0\text{,}\) we certainly have \(d(x,x) = 0 \lt \varepsilon\text{.}\)

  2. For every \(\varepsilon>0\text{,}\) the sentence

    Sentences of the form \((\exists s \in \varnothing)(\phi(s))\) are always false, no matter what \(\phi(s)\) says!

    \((\exists s \in \varnothing)(d(x,s)\lt \varepsilon)\) is false.

  3. If \(x\) is close to \(S_1 \cup \dots \cup S_n\text{,}\) then for every natural number

    In this text, \(\NN\) will denote the natural numbers \(\{0,1,\dots\}\text{,}\) and \(\NN^{\ast}\) will denote the natural numbers \(\{1,2,\dots\}\text{.}\)

    \(m \in \NN^{\ast}\text{,}\) we may select \(s_m \in S_1 \cup \dots \cup S_n\) such that \(d(x, s_m) \lt 1/m\text{.}\) By the pigeonhole principle, there exists an \(i\) such that the set \(\{ m \in \NN^{\ast} : s_m \in S_i \}\) is infinite. Consequently, for any \(\varepsilon>0\text{,}\) there exists a natural number \(m\geq 1\) such that \(s_m \in S_i\) and \(1/m \lt \varepsilon\text{.}\) In particular

    \begin{equation*} d(x,s_m) \lt 1/m \lt \varepsilon\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Thus \(x\) is close to \(S_i\text{.}\) Conversely, if \(x\) is close to some \(S_i\text{,}\) then for every \(\varepsilon >0\text{,}\) there exists \(s \in S_i \subseteq S_1 \cup \dots \cup S_n\) such that \(d(x,s) \lt \varepsilon\text{,}\) so \(x\) is close to \(S_1 \cup \dots \cup S_n\text{.}\)

  4. If \(x\) is close to \(\tau_X(S)\text{,}\) then for any \(\varepsilon >0\text{,}\) we may select \(t\in \tau_X(S)\) such that \(d(x,t) \lt \varepsilon/2\text{.}\) Since \(t\) is close to \(S\text{,}\) we may select \(s \in S\) such that \(d(t, s) \lt \varepsilon/2\text{.}\) Now by the triangle inequality,

    \begin{equation*} d(x, s) \leq d(x, t) + d(t, s) \lt \varepsilon\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Thus \(x\) is close to \(S\text{.}\)
