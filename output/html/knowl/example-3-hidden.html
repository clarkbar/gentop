• For any subspace \(X \subseteq \RR^n\text{,}\) the subset \(X \subseteq X\) is both open and closed.

    This is sometimes a source of confusion — or at least irritation. One may expect that “open” and “closed” are opposites, but they are not: there are subsets that are neither, and there are subsets that are both. Instead, they are complementary notions.

  • For any subspace \(X \subseteq \RR^n\text{,}\) the subset \(\varnothing \subseteq X\) is both open and closed.

  • In \(\RR\) itself, the subset \([0,1] \subset \RR\) is closed but not open.

  • In \(\RR\text{,}\) the subset \(\left]0,1\right[ \subset \RR\) is open but not closed.

  • In \(\RR\text{,}\) the subsets \(\left[0,1\right[\) and \(\left]0,1\right]\) are neither open nor closed.

  • In the subspace \(\left]0, +\infty\right[ \subseteq \RR\text{,}\) the subset \(\left]0,1\right] \subseteq \left]0, +\infty\right[\) is closed but not open.

  • In the subspace \(\left[0, +\infty\right[ \subseteq \RR\text{,}\) the subset \(\left[0,1\right[ \subseteq \left[0, +\infty\right[\) is open but not closed.
