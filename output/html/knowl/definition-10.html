Definition 2.2.5.

Suppose \(\tau_1\) and \(\tau_2\) are two topologies on the same set \(X\text{.}\) Then we say that \(\tau_1\) is finer than \(\tau_2\) — and that \(\tau_2\) is coarser than \(\tau_1\) — if the identity map on \(X\) is continuous as a map

\begin{equation*} (X,\tau_1) (X,\tau_2)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Hence if \(\tau_1\) is finer than \(\tau_2\text{,}\) then the closure of a set relative to \(\tau_1\) is contained in its closure relative to \(\tau_2\text{.}\) In particular, the topology \(\tau_1\) has more closed sets than the topology \(\tau_2\text{.}\) Forming complements, we deduce also that the topology \(\tau_1\) has more open sets than the topology \(\tau_2\text{.}\)

There are two extremes: the finest topology on a set is the discrete topology, and the coarsest is the chaotic topology.
